Black Excellence in Los Angeles-Based Movies
As we return to USC, let's take a moment to reflect on the powerful history and stories that have come from the streets, neighborhoods, and dreams of this city. From 1991 to 2025, Los Angeles has been home to some of the most influential films that highlight Black culture, resilience, and brilliance. These movies don't just entertain us, they shape how we understand the complexities of life, love, and identity within one of the world's most diverse cities.
One of Them Days (2025)

     One of Them Days, produced by the Issa Rae, is a comedy that'll have you in stitches from start to finish. The film captures the chaos of life's most relatable, awkward, and hilarious moments, where everything seems to go wrong, but in the best possible way.

     With sharp, witty dialogue and a simple yet effective plot, it's a comedy of errors with surprising heart. Keke Palmer and SZA bring incredible chemistry and flawless comedic timing to the screen, making their dynamic effortless and fun.

     A feel-good, laugh-out-loud experience, One of Them Days is a reminder of why comedy is making such a strong comeback in 2025. If you're looking for a good laugh, this movie is a must-watch.

Cast:
Director: Lawrence Lamont
Writer: Syreeta Singleton
Stars: Keke Palmer, SZA, Vanessa Bell Calloway
Boyz n the Hood (1991)

     I heard a lot of great things about Boyz n the Hood, but I didn't watch it until recently, and it was the best decision I ever made.

     The film's emotional depth and raw portrayal of the struggles of being a Black man in a poor, gang-ridden neighborhood is powerful.

     Without spoiling anything, it's a must-watch to understand gang life in America. This movie will always be relevant, it's a real eye-opener and a necessary dose of reality.

Cast:
Director: John Singleton
Writer: John Singleton
Stars: Cuba Gooding Jr., Laurence Fishburne, Angela Bassett, Ice Cube

Love and Basketball (2000)

     Love and Basketball is a timeless story about love, dreams, and the challenges of balancing both. It follows Monica and Quincy, childhood friends turned romantic interests, who navigate their careers and personal lives while sharing a deep passion for basketball.

     One of my favorite moments is when Monica's mom, Camille, tells her that when she said Quincy could do better, she was talking about Monica, showing just how much her mom's opinion meant to her.

     I've watched this movie so many times, and it just gets better with age. The way it explores the complexities of love, ambition, and sacrifice makes it a film that stays with you long after the credits roll.

Cast:
Director: Gina Prince-Bythewood
Writer: Gina Prince-Bythewood
Stars: Sanaa Lathan, Omar Epps, Glenndon Chatman

Friday (1995)

     I never get tired of watching Friday! Ice Cube and DJ Pooh created unforgettable characters like Smokey, the cartoonish sidekick, and Deebo, the perpetual bully, alongside Craig's dad, whose wisdom and calm demeanor stand in sharp contrast to Craig's youthful chaos.

     The movie's full of quotable lines, with "Bye, Felicia" being a standout. And of course, there's that DAMNNN image you can hear, just seeing it brings the perfect mix of humor and now common knowledge

     Friday isn't just a movie, it's a cultural staple that still hits every time.

Cast:
Director: F. Gary Gary
Writer: Ice Cube, DJ Pooh
Stars: Ice Cube, Chris Tucker, Nia Long
